Vitrectomy

What is Vitrectomy?

Vitrectomy is a surgical procedure that involves removing the vitreous gel from the middle of the eye to treat various eye problems. This surgery allows the eye surgeon to access the back of the eye for treatments such as repairing retinal detachments, removing scar tissue, and treating macular holes. The goal is to restore or improve vision impaired by these conditions.

Common Reasons for Vitrectomy

The procedure is commonly performed to address issues such as retinal detachment, diabetic retinopathy complications, vitreous hemorrhage, macular hole, and epiretinal membrane. It may also be necessary to remove foreign bodies from the eye or to treat severe eye infections.

Why Need Vitrectomy

Vitrectomy is needed when less invasive treatments are not adequate to correct serious eye conditions that can lead to vision loss. By removing the vitreous gel and treating the underlying issue directly, vitrectomy can significantly improve the chances of restoring vision and preventing further eye damage.

Before and After Surgery

Before Surgery: Patients will undergo a comprehensive eye examination. The surgeon will discuss the risks and benefits of the surgery, and may advise stopping certain medications or recommend preoperative tests.

After Surgery: The recovery process varies, depending on the underlying condition treated and the complexity of the surgery. Patients may need to use antibiotic and anti-inflammatory eye drops and avoid certain activities during recovery. Postoperative appointments are crucial to monitor the healing process and to adjust treatment as necessary.
