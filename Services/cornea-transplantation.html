Cornea Transplantation

What is Cornea Transplantation?

Cornea transplantation, also known as keratoplasty, is a surgical procedure that replaces part or all of a damaged or diseased cornea with healthy corneal tissue from a donor. This operation can restore vision, reduce pain, and improve the appearance of a damaged or diseased cornea.

Common Reasons for Cornea Transplantation

Reasons for undergoing cornea transplantation include corneal failure due to previous eye surgeries, keratoconus (a condition where the cornea thins and bulges outward), corneal scarring from injuries or infections, and hereditary conditions like Fuchs' dystrophy.

Why Cornea Transplantation is Needed

Cornea transplantation is needed when vision cannot be corrected satisfactorily with glasses or contact lenses and is not amenable to other treatments. The procedure aims to restore functional vision, relieve pain, and improve the overall quality of life for individuals with corneal blindness or severe visual impairment.

Before and After Surgery

Before Surgery: A thorough evaluation, including measurements of the eye, is performed to ensure a good match with the donor tissue. Patients may need to undergo preoperative tests and discontinue wearing contact lenses for a period before the surgery.

After Surgery: Recovery from cornea transplantation can vary, with complete healing often taking up to a year or longer. Patients will need to use prescribed eye drops to prevent infection and rejection of the donor cornea and attend regular follow-up appointments to monitor healing and visual recovery.
